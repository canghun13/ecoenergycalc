Electric vs Gas Dryer: Cost Calculator for Your Rates

9 min read · June 2026

Gas dryers typically cost less per load in most U.S. regions — but the answer depends on your local rates. Enter your rates to find the cheapest option for your home.

Annual Cost & Payback

Electric dryer: 5,000W (standard) or 4,000W (ENERGY STAR). Gas dryer: 700W motor + 0.22 therms/hr gas. Heat pump dryer: 2,000W.

Gas vs Electric Dryers: Operating Cost Comparison

A typical electric dryer uses 5 kWh per load. At 13¢/kWh, each load costs $0.065. Gas dryers use roughly 0.22 therms per load. At $1.40/therm, each load costs $0.031. Gas is consistently cheaper per load when these national average prices apply — roughly 50–60% cheaper per cycle. At 5 loads per week × 52 weeks = 260 loads/year: electric costs $16.90/year, gas costs $8.06/year. The $8.84/year difference is modest — the annual savings from switching gas to electric are small enough that the decision should be driven by installation infrastructure, not operating cost alone.

The Real Question: Installation Infrastructure

If you have a gas line at the laundry location, a gas dryer makes sense — lower operating cost, no electrical upgrade needed, and slightly faster drying times. If you only have a 240V electrical outlet, an electric dryer is the obvious choice — installing gas service to the laundry room costs $500–$1,500 or more depending on distance from the main line. The operating cost savings never justify a gas line installation. The decision usually makes itself based on existing infrastructure — not by running cost comparisons.

Heat Pump Dryers: The Third Option Worth Considering

Heat pump dryers recirculate air through a heat exchanger rather than exhausting it, using 40–50% less electricity than conventional electric dryers (approximately 2.5–3 kWh per load vs. 5 kWh). At 13¢/kWh, heat pump dryer cost is $0.033/load — essentially matching gas at current national rates. The 25C federal appliance tax credit (10%, up to $150) helps offset the $800–$1,200 price premium over standard electric. Heat pump dryers also don't require an exhaust duct — useful for homes where venting to exterior is difficult. The main trade-off: longer drying cycle (60–90 minutes vs. 45 minutes for conventional).

Ventless Electric: A Practical Option for Apartments

Condensing electric dryers (non-heat pump ventless) are common in Europe and growing in U.S. apartments where exterior venting is impractical. They use slightly more electricity than heat pump dryers but less than vented electric, and can be installed anywhere with a 240V outlet and drain connection. For apartment renters or condo owners with laundry hookups but no exterior vent, ventless electric is the primary option — and at 3.5–4 kWh/load, it costs $0.046–$0.052/load, still cheaper than gas in higher electricity rate markets like New England or California.
