The Real Competition: E-Bike vs. Second Car

The meaningful financial comparison isn't e-bike vs. your primary car — it's e-bike vs. owning a second car for shorter trips. The average American household spends $12,000–$15,000/year on vehicle ownership (AAA, 2024). If an e-bike can replace a second car for a two-vehicle household, the annual savings are enormous. A quality e-bike costs $1,500–$4,000 with roughly $200–$400/year in maintenance. Eliminating a second car saves insurance ($1,200–$2,000/year), depreciation ($2,000–$5,000/year), registration, and fuel — easily $5,000–$8,000 annually.

Commute Range and Practicality

Most e-bikes provide 20–60 miles of assisted range per charge (depending on assist level and terrain). A commute of 5–15 miles each way is the sweet spot for e-bike practicality. Beyond 20 miles each way, cargo capacity, weather exposure, and physical exertion become limiting factors. The practical advantages compound: no parking costs, no traffic delay in dense urban areas, and the ability to arrive at work without sweating through work clothes. Cities like Portland, Minneapolis, and Denver — which have invested in separated bike infrastructure — have seen e-bike commuting grow 50–100% year over year.

Energy Cost: The Most Lopsided Comparison in Transportation

An e-bike uses roughly 10–20 Wh per mile (0.01–0.02 kWh/mile). At 13¢/kWh, that's $0.001–$0.003 per mile. A 30 MPG car at $3.90/gallon costs $0.13/mile. The e-bike is 43–130× cheaper per mile in energy costs. Over 3,000 commuting miles per year (a typical urban commuter), an e-bike costs $3–$9 in electricity. The car costs $390. This energy cost advantage is so large it dominates any cost comparison for short to medium commutes — no other transportation mode comes close.

What an E-Bike Can't Replace

E-bikes don't work well in heavy rain (you and cargo get wet), aren't practical for grocery runs over 20 lbs without a cargo bike, and face real safety risks on roads without bike infrastructure. They also require secure parking at the destination — theft is a significant concern for unattended bikes in urban areas. For households with children requiring transportation, school drop-offs, or frequent large cargo needs, an e-bike complements rather than replaces a car. Even so, replacing 30% of car trips with an e-bike saves $1,500–$3,000/year in fuel and vehicle wear.
