Heat Pump Dryer vs Electric: Payback Calculator

9 min read · June 2026

Heat pump dryers use 40–50% less electricity than standard electric dryers. Whether the upgrade makes sense depends on your electricity rate and how often you do laundry. Calculate your specific payback below.

Heat Pump Dryer Technology: How It Works

Conventional electric dryers work by heating air with an electric element, blowing it through clothes, and exhausting the moisture-laden air to the exterior. This wastes the thermal energy in that exhaust air. Heat pump dryers recirculate air through a closed loop: the heat pump extracts moisture from the air, condenses it to water (which drains), reheats the drier air, and returns it through the drum. This recirculation recovers 50–60% of the thermal energy that a conventional dryer exhausts, reducing electricity use per load from approximately 5 kWh to 2.5–3 kWh.

Annual Savings Calculation

At 260 loads/year (5 loads/week): conventional electric at 5 kWh/load uses 1,300 kWh/year; heat pump dryer at 2.7 kWh/load uses 702 kWh/year. Savings: 598 kWh/year. At 13¢/kWh = $77.7/year. At 20¢/kWh (California): $119.6/year. Note: clothes dryers were never eligible for the federal Section 25C tax credit (that credit covered HVAC heat pumps, heat pump water heaters, insulation, and windows only) — the $840 figure sometimes quoted for heat pump dryers is a separate HEEHRA/HEAR income-qualified state rebate program, not a tax credit, and it's still rolling out in many states in 2026 (not expired). If you don't qualify by income or your state hasn't launched the program yet, a $1,000 heat pump dryer costs the full $1,000 vs. a $500 conventional electric — a $500 premium. At $78/year savings, payback is roughly 6.4 years. At California rates, payback is about 4.2 years.

No Exhaust Duct Required: A Practical Advantage

Conventional dryers require a 4-inch duct to the exterior — a significant installation constraint in condos, basements, or homes where the laundry room doesn't have exterior wall access. Heat pump dryers require only a 240V outlet and a drain connection (or a condensate collection container that you empty periodically). This opens up laundry room placement options that don't exist for vented dryers, and eliminates the fire hazard associated with lint buildup in dryer ducts — which causes approximately 2,900 U.S. structure fires annually according to the NFPA.

The Trade-Offs You Should Know Before Buying

Heat pump dryers have three meaningful disadvantages: (1) Longer cycle times — typically 60–90 minutes vs. 45 minutes for conventional. If laundry speed is important (large household, multiple daily loads), this matters. (2) Higher upfront cost — expect to pay $800–$1,400 for quality models (Bosch, LG, Miele) vs. $400–$600 for conventional electric. (3) Lower maximum temperature — heat pump dryers max out at around 140°F vs. 150–175°F for conventional, which means some items (certain cotton bedding that calls for "high heat") may take longer or feel less fluffy. None of these are dealbreakers for most households, but worth knowing before purchase.
