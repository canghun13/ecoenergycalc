What "Battery Storage" Actually Means for Your Decision

When people ask "solar vs. battery storage," they usually mean: should I add a battery to my existing or planned solar system, or skip it? The real question is what problem you're trying to solve. A battery solves three distinct problems — backup power during outages, time-of-use arbitrage (charging cheap, discharging during expensive peak hours), and maximizing self-consumption in states that have reduced net metering compensation. If your utility offers full retail net metering and outages are rare, a battery's financial return is thin. If you're in California (NEM 3.0), Hawaii, or another market with reduced export rates, batteries become economically compelling.

Payback Math in a Reduced Net Metering World

Under NEM 3.0 in California, solar exports are compensated at roughly 5¢/kWh rather than the retail rate of 28–35¢/kWh. A home-battery (10–13.5 kWh usable) that stores midday solar and discharges during 4–9 PM peak hours saves the full retail rate on every kWh shifted. At 10 kWh shifted daily × $0.28 × 365 = $1,022/year. A single Tesla Powerwall 3 costs ~$12,000 installed. That's an 11-12 year payback — before accounting for backup value. In markets with strong time-of-use differentials, returns are better.

Backup Power: The Value That's Hard to Quantify

For many homeowners, the primary driver for battery storage isn't the financial return — it's resilience. A single Powerwall provides 13.5 kWh, enough to run essential loads (refrigerator, lights, phone charging, some HVAC) for 12–24 hours during an outage. Paired with solar, a battery can extend backup indefinitely in sunny conditions while the grid is down. If you've experienced multi-day outages or have medical equipment dependence, the non-financial value of backup is real and deeply personal.

Should You Add Battery Now or Later?

Installing battery at the same time as solar saves on installation costs — one contractor mobilization, one permitting process, simplified interconnection. But technology is improving and prices are falling. A reasonable strategy: install solar now with a battery-ready inverter (Enphase IQ8, SolarEdge with backup), and add battery within 2–5 years once you understand your usage patterns and export compensation rates. This avoids committing to today's battery prices for a product category still seeing rapid improvement.
