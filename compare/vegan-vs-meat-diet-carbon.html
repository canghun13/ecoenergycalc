The Numbers: Diet Carbon Footprint by Category

Food production accounts for roughly 26% of global greenhouse gas emissions. Within that, livestock farming generates approximately 14.5% of global emissions (FAO data) — more than all transportation combined. Individual diet carbon footprints vary enormously by choices: beef at 27 kg CO₂e/kg of protein produced is the highest-impact common food. By comparison: tofu = 2.2 kg CO₂e/kg protein, lentils = 0.9 kg CO₂e/kg protein, chicken = 6.9 kg CO₂e/kg protein, pork = 7.6 kg CO₂e/kg protein. The difference between a beef-heavy diet and a plant-based diet is approximately 1.5–2.5 metric tons CO₂e per year per person — comparable to eliminating a car for most commuters.

The "Vegan vs. Meat" Framing Misses Most of the Variation

A more useful framing is: beef consumption is by far the dominant food carbon variable, and dairy follows. A diet that eliminates beef but includes chicken, fish, and dairy has roughly 75% lower food carbon footprint than a beef-heavy diet. Vegan vs. heavy beef-eater represents a 200–300% footprint difference; omnivore vs. beef-avoider (chicken and fish only) is still a 50–80% reduction. The practical implication: reducing beef specifically — not eliminating all animal products — captures most of the food carbon benefit. This is actionable for many people who find full veganism impractical.

Land Use: The Second Major Impact Category

Beyond carbon, animal agriculture's land footprint is striking. Producing 1 calorie of beef requires approximately 20× more land than producing 1 calorie of grain. Globally, 77% of agricultural land is used for livestock (including feed crop production) while producing only 18% of global calorie supply. The land efficiency argument for plant-based diets is arguably stronger than the carbon argument — land freed from beef production and rewilded or reforested would sequester additional carbon, improving the climate math further beyond direct emission reductions from diet change alone.

Local and Seasonal: Meaningful but Smaller Than You Think

"Eat local" is widely cited as a climate strategy, but transportation represents only approximately 6% of food system emissions. The production method matters far more than the shipping distance. Flying fresh produce from Chile or Kenya (air freight) does have significant carbon impact. But shipping food by sea or truck — how most imported food arrives — has minimal carbon footprint per kg. A locally grown beef steak has a larger carbon footprint than imported lentils from Canada. The origin matters less than what you eat; the food category is the primary carbon driver, not geography.
