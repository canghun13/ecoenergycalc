How Tankless Water Heaters Work — and Where They Fall Short

Tankless (on-demand) water heaters heat water only when needed, eliminating standby losses from keeping a tank warm 24/7. They're rated by flow rate (gallons per minute) rather than storage capacity. A whole-home gas tankless unit typically outputs 7–11 GPM, sufficient for 2–3 simultaneous hot water uses (shower + dishwasher, for example). The limitation: flow rate at the fixture is constrained by the heater's capacity; on cold inlet-water days (groundwater temperatures drop to 40–45°F in winter in northern states), a heater rated for 8 GPM at a 35°F rise may only deliver 5 GPM at a 65°F rise. Sizing for your coldest winter conditions matters enormously.

Standby Loss: The Actual Cost Difference

A 50-gallon natural gas tank water heater loses roughly 0.5–1.0 therm/day in standby heat — $0.70–$1.40/day or $255–$511/year just to keep water hot. A 40-gallon electric tank heater loses about 1.0–2.0 kWh/day in standby — $0.13–$0.26/day or $47–$95/year. Gas tankless heaters eliminate most standby loss (a small electronic ignition has minimal standby consumption). The standby savings are real and meaningful — especially for gas water heaters where standby loss is higher in absolute terms.

Payback Period: The Honest Calculation

A 50-gallon gas tank water heater costs $600–$900 installed. A whole-home gas tankless runs $1,500–$3,000 installed. At $400/year in standby savings (mid-range estimate), payback on a $2,000 price premium is 5 years. Actual household savings are lower because tankless unit users often run longer showers since hot water doesn't "run out," partially offsetting efficiency gains. Independent studies put real-world tankless gas savings at $70–$150/year vs. tank, implying 13–29 year paybacks. The payback is often longer than marketing materials suggest.

The Better Alternative: Heat Pump Water Heaters

Before comparing tank vs. tankless, consider heat pump water heaters (HPWH). These use electricity but operate at COP 3.0–4.0, meaning they deliver 3–4 units of heat per unit of electricity. At 13¢/kWh and COP 3.5, a HPWH costs $0.037/kBTU delivered heat — cheaper than gas tank or gas tankless. The IRA's $300 federal tax credit (up to 30%) applies to HPWHs. A 50-gallon HPWH costs $800–$1,200 before credit. Annual operating cost: $200–$300 — about half the cost of a standard gas tank heater. This is the option that often makes the most financial sense in 2025 for households on electricity.
