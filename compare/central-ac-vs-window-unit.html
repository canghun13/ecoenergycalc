Central AC vs Window AC: Cost Calculator

9 min read · June 2026

The right choice depends on how much of your home you need to cool. Central AC wins for whole-home cooling. Window units are cheaper for single rooms. Enter your details to compare.

Cooling Cost Comparison

Annual Cooling Cost

Central AC (SEER 16): 500W per 100 sq ft. Window units: 1,000W per 400 sq ft (EER 10). Mini-split (SEER 20): 350W per 100 sq ft. Installation: central AC $5,500, window units $300 each, mini-split $3,500 per zone.

When Window Units Win on Economics

For homes that only need cooling in 1–3 rooms, window air conditioners are genuinely hard to beat economically. A 6,000 BTU window unit costs $150–$300 and consumes 500–700W. Running it 8 hours/day for 3 months costs $35–$65 at 13¢/kWh. Central AC installation for a 2,000 sq ft home runs $3,500–$7,500 for the equipment and $1,000–$3,000 for ductwork if not present. If you're cooling a 1-bedroom apartment or single room, the payback comparison isn't even close — you'd need decades for central AC to pay back on lower operating costs alone.

SEER Ratings Explained — What Actually Matters for Your Bill

SEER (Seasonal Energy Efficiency Ratio) is the efficiency metric for air conditioners. Federal minimum is SEER 14 (14 BTUs of cooling per watt-hour of electricity). High-efficiency units reach SEER 20–26. A SEER 20 unit uses 30% less electricity than SEER 14 for the same cooling output. On a $800/year central AC electricity bill (2,000 sq ft, hot climate), moving from SEER 14 to SEER 20 saves $240/year. The premium for SEER 20 over SEER 14 in equipment cost runs $500–$1,500, yielding a 2–6 year payback. In hot climates with long cooling seasons (Texas, Florida, Arizona), high-SEER equipment always pencils out.

Ductwork: The Hidden Cost of Central AC

Homes without existing ductwork face a steep additional cost for central AC: $3,000–$8,000 to add ducts, plus the unit itself. In these homes, mini-split systems (ductless heat pumps) often make more sense. A 3-zone mini-split system ($8,000–$15,000 installed) provides both cooling and heating, eliminates duct losses (ducts in unconditioned attics can lose 20–30% of conditioned air), and qualifies for the 30% federal heat pump tax credit. The ductless vs. central decision often hinges entirely on whether ductwork already exists in the home.

Humidity Control: Central AC's Hidden Advantage

Central air conditioning conditions the entire home volume, including reducing humidity throughout. In humid climates (Southeast, Gulf Coast, Mid-Atlantic), humidity control is as important as temperature for comfort. Window units condition only the room they're in; humidity from unconditioned rooms permeates throughout. If you've ever run multiple window units and still felt clammy, this is why — the unconditioned hallways and adjacent rooms are exporting humidity into your cooled space. For whole-home humidity control in humid climates, central AC or a whole-home dehumidifier is necessary.
