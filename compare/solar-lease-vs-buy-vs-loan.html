Solar Lease vs Buy vs Loan

9 min read · June 2026

The financing method you choose determines whether you own the system, qualify for tax credits, and how much you save over 25 years. Enter your details below to compare all three options with real numbers.

Compare Your Solar Financing Options

25-Year Comparison

Cash and loan scenarios assume 30% federal ITC. Loan term: 20 years. Annual solar production degradation: 0.5%/year. Electricity rate escalation: 2%/year.

Cash Purchase: Highest Long-Term Return, Highest Barrier

Paying cash for a solar system gives you the best 25-year economics. You capture the full 30% federal Investment Tax Credit (ITC) immediately, own the system outright, and every dollar of electricity production flows directly into your pocket. On a 6 kW system at $3/watt, that's roughly $18,000 installed — minus a $5,400 ITC = $12,600 effective cost. Most homeowners in mid-sun states recover that in 6–9 years and earn $25,000–$40,000 net over 25 years depending on electricity rate escalation.

The catch: you need the capital. And you must have sufficient federal tax liability to use the ITC — it's a credit, not a refund. If your annual tax bill is $3,000 and the ITC is $5,400, you'll carry the remainder forward, but it takes time to capture fully.

Solar Loans: Zero Down, You Still Own the System

Solar-specific loans have become the dominant financing vehicle. The most important fact: you still claim the 30% ITC, which you then apply to the loan principal in year one. With a 6% solar loan over 20 years on a $12,600 net-cost system, monthly payments run about $90–100. Net 25-year savings are typically $5,000–$15,000 less than cash purchase (the interest paid), but you deploy zero capital upfront.

Watch for dealer fees buried in solar loans. Many "0% APR" or "low-rate" solar loans charge the installer a 15–30% dealer fee, which installers recoup by quoting higher system prices. Always get a separate cash quote and compare.

Leases and PPAs: Savings Without Ownership

Under a lease or Power Purchase Agreement (PPA), a third party owns your system. You pay a fixed monthly fee (lease) or a per-kWh rate slightly below retail (PPA). The critical drawback: the installer captures the 30% ITC, not you. Your long-term savings are substantially lower — typically $5,000–$15,000 less than a comparable loan scenario over 25 years.

Leases make sense in specific cases: you have no federal tax liability, you can't qualify for a loan, or you want production-performance guarantees with no maintenance responsibility. Some homeowners genuinely value the simplicity of a fixed monthly payment with the installer responsible for all repairs.

The Home Sale Complication: What Nobody Tells You

A leased system creates an obligation that must transfer to the buyer at home sale. Some buyers refuse to assume lease payments; others face credit qualification requirements set by the leasing company. This doesn't block sales, but it can slow them and narrow your buyer pool. Owned systems (cash or loan-paid-off) add documented value: studies show owned solar adds roughly $4/watt to home sale price in most markets. Leased systems add less — or are treated as a liability by cautious buyers.
