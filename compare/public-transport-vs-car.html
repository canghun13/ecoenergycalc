The True Cost of Car Commuting

AAA's annual "Your Driving Costs" study (2024) puts the average cost of driving a medium sedan at $0.58/mile including depreciation, fuel, insurance, maintenance, and financing. At 30 miles round-trip daily × 250 commute days = 7,500 miles/year, commuting by car costs roughly $4,350/year in total vehicle costs attributable to the commute. Monthly transit passes in major U.S. cities range from $100 (smaller cities) to $132 (NYC Metro) to $99 (Chicago). Annual transit cost: $1,200–$1,600. The financial case for transit commuting is clear for anyone who can actually use it effectively.

But Depreciation Skews Everything

Car ownership costs are largely fixed — you pay insurance, registration, and depreciation whether you drive 5,000 or 15,000 miles. If you already own a car and the marginal cost of driving to work is just fuel ($0.13/mile at national averages), the transit vs. car math changes. At $0.13/mile marginal cost for fuel, a 7,500-mile annual commute costs $975 in fuel — potentially less than an annual transit pass. The financial benefit of transit over driving only becomes compelling when you can eliminate a car entirely, saving $5,000–$12,000/year in fixed ownership costs.

Time: The Variable Nobody Prices Correctly

Transit commutes are often slower than driving in U.S. cities — 30–80% longer in most metro areas with limited dedicated bus or rail infrastructure. But transit time can be productive: reading, working, or resting time that driving doesn't allow. Studies on commuter wellbeing consistently show that transit commuters report lower stress than drivers, even when commutes are longer. The ability to use a phone, read, or work during a 40-minute train commute has real value that raw time comparisons miss. For knowledge workers, a train commute may actually produce more effective working time than a shorter but stressful drive.

Carbon: Transit's Clearest Advantage

The average car emits 0.35 kg CO₂/mile (EPA 2024 fleet average). Heavy rail transit emits 0.07–0.14 kg CO₂/passenger mile (depending on grid mix and occupancy). Bus rapid transit ranges from 0.06–0.18 kg CO₂/passenger mile. Even diesel buses at average U.S. occupancy (about 20 passengers) emit less per passenger than a solo car commuter. For households motivated by carbon reduction, replacing a car commute with transit — even imperfect transit — typically cuts commute-related emissions by 60–80%.
