The Physics: Why LEDs Use Less Energy for the Same Light

Incandescent bulbs produce light by heating a filament to approximately 2,700°C — a process where roughly 90% of energy becomes heat and only 10% becomes visible light. This isn't a design flaw that can be engineered away; it's thermodynamics. CFLs use fluorescent tube technology (excited gas plus phosphor coating) that converts roughly 25% of energy to visible light. LEDs use semiconductor electroluminescence that converts 35–50% of energy directly to light. The practical result: a 60W incandescent, 13W CFL, and 9W LED all produce about 800 lumens (equivalent visible light output). The LED uses 85% less electricity than the incandescent for identical light output.

Lifespan: Where the Real Savings Accumulate

Incandescent bulbs last approximately 1,000 hours. CFLs last 8,000–10,000 hours. Quality LEDs last 15,000–25,000 hours. At 3 hours/day usage, an incandescent needs replacement annually. A CFL lasts 7–9 years. An LED lasts 14–23 years. Over 25 years: you buy 25 incandescent bulbs ($0.75 each = $18.75), about 3 CFLs ($3 each = $9), or 1–2 LEDs ($3–5 total). Multiply this across 20 bulbs in a home and bulb replacement costs alone add up: $375 for incandescent vs. $80 for LED over 25 years — not including the time cost of frequent replacements or replacement while standing on ladders.

Light Quality: LEDs Have Caught Up Completely

Early LEDs had reputation problems: harsh color, poor dimming, flickering, directional light. 2020 and later LED technology has largely resolved these issues. Quality LEDs now achieve CRI (Color Rendering Index) of 90+ — matching or exceeding incandescent (CRI approximately 100). Warm white (2700K) LEDs are visually indistinguishable from incandescent in most settings. Dimming compatibility has improved: most quality LEDs dim smoothly with compatible LED dimmers (not old incandescent dimmers, which often cause flickering). Energy Star certification is the fastest quality screen — it requires minimum CRI 80, specific lumen maintenance over life, and dimming performance standards.

What Happens to CFLs at End of Life

CFLs contain 3–5 mg of mercury per bulb — not dangerous in normal use, but requiring proper disposal. Home Depot and Lowes accept CFL recycling; municipal hazardous waste programs also take them. Most states prohibit CFL landfill disposal. If you have CFLs in your home now, let them run out their rated life (they last 8+ years when left on for full cycles), then replace with LEDs. Don't throw CFLs away intact — they'll eventually break in landfill and release mercury. CFL manufacturing has largely been phased out; any comparison you face today is managing existing inventory rather than making new purchase decisions.
