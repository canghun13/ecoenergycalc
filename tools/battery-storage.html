Do You Need a Home Battery?

Home batteries are most valuable when: (1) you have solar panels and want to use power after dark, (2) your utility has time-of-use rates with expensive evening peaks, or (3) you want backup power during outages.

Grid Outages and Energy Resilience

Beyond economics, backup power resilience is a major reason homeowners invest in battery storage. Grid-tied solar panels without batteries shut off during a power outage — a safety requirement to prevent backfeeding electricity to utility workers. Adding battery storage allows your solar system to continue powering your home during an outage, running essential loads like refrigerators, medical equipment, lighting, and device charging.

The value of resilience depends heavily on your location. In areas prone to hurricanes, wildfires, ice storms, or aging grid infrastructure, power outages can last days. In areas with stable grids and rare outages, the financial case for a battery may be harder to justify on resilience alone.

Time-of-Use Rates and Battery Arbitrage

One of the strongest economic cases for battery storage is time-of-use (TOU) electricity rates. Under TOU pricing, electricity costs significantly more during peak demand hours — typically 4–9 PM on weekdays — than during off-peak hours overnight and on weekends. The peak-to-off-peak price difference can be 2–3x in some utility territories.

A battery system can charge overnight at cheap off-peak rates and discharge during expensive peak hours, saving $300–700 per year depending on your rate structure and usage. Combined with solar panels — which generate excess power midday that can be stored for evening use — batteries can shift a substantial portion of your electricity consumption away from peak pricing. If you're on flat-rate pricing, the TOU arbitrage benefit doesn't apply, and the economics for batteries rest primarily on resilience value.

Battery Chemistry and What to Look For

Most residential batteries use lithium iron phosphate (LFP) chemistry, which offers long cycle lives (typically 4,000+ charge cycles), good safety characteristics, and is well-suited for daily cycling. Key performance specs to compare when evaluating batteries: usable capacity (kWh), continuous power output (kW — how many appliances it can run simultaneously), round-trip efficiency (what percentage of stored energy you get back, typically 85–95%), and warranty terms — typically 10 years with a guaranteed minimum end-of-warranty capacity.

For sizing: essential backup only during a typical 8–12 hour outage generally requires 5–10 kWh. Full-home backup or multi-day resilience may need 15–30 kWh or more. Most installers recommend pairing battery storage with solar panels so the panels can recharge the battery each day, enabling extended off-grid operation during prolonged outages.

Battery product prices and specifications change frequently. Verify current pricing and availability with installers before making purchasing decisions.
